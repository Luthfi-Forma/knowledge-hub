An RPPLH — Rencana Perlindungan dan Pengelolaan Lingkungan Hidup,
Indonesia's statutory Environmental Protection and Management Plan — is the
document a province is required to produce before it can claim to be
managing its environment deliberately rather than reactively. South Papua,
one of Indonesia's newest provinces, needed one at the same time the
national Food Estate program — a large-scale push to convert land to
agricultural production — was actively expanding into it. That timing made
the plan more than a routine compliance document: it was the province's
main opportunity to put an evidence base on record before development
decisions got made around it.

## Inventorying vulnerability at province scale

The core of the work was a province-wide ecosystem-vulnerability inventory
built in Google Earth Engine, combining land cover, terrain, and hydrological
layers into a composite vulnerability classification across South Papua's
full extent. At provincial scale, hand-digitizing or field-surveying every
unit of land isn't feasible in the timeframe an RPPLH is produced on, so the
inventory leaned on remote-sensing layers already available at that scale,
cross-checked and organized in Excel alongside the spatial outputs to keep
the classification auditable rather than a black box.

## Locating what the Food Estate program would affect

The more consequential piece layered the Food Estate program's known and
planned development footprint against two things: the ecosystem-vulnerability
classification, and — separately — a mapping of sacred and customary
(adat) land across the province. South Papua's customary land isn't
incidental to how the region is governed; it's central to it, and land that
carries customary or sacred status but isn't formally reflected in
development planning is exactly where social-conflict risk shows up later,
after commitments have already been made. Flagging those overlaps inside the
RPPLH — rather than leaving them to surface during implementation — was the
point of including this layer at all.

## Output

The RPPLH was delivered as a technical planning document to South Papua's
provincial government and the Ministry of Environment and Forestry,
combining the vulnerability inventory with the customary-land overlay as
inputs the province can weigh before approving further Food Estate
expansion. Like any RPPLH, its authority comes from being adopted into
planning decisions, not from the document itself — but a vulnerability
inventory and a customary-land flag that didn't previously exist in any
consolidated, spatial form now do.
