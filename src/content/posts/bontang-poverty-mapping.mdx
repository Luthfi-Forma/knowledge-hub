P3KE — Pendataan Keluarga Sejahtera, Indonesia's national registry for
targeting extreme-poverty programs — gives every region a household-level
list of families flagged as extremely poor. What it doesn't give a local
government is a map: P3KE ships as tabular data, tied to administrative
addresses, with no built-in way to see where those households actually
cluster or what conditions surround them. This project took Bontang City's
P3KE records and turned them into something a planning team could actually
use — a spatial picture of extreme poverty across the city's sub-districts.

## From address list to hotspot map

The first step was preparing and updating the raw P3KE target data —
checking household records against current administrative boundaries,
resolving addresses that didn't geocode cleanly, and joining the cleaned
data to sub-district and kelurahan polygons in ArcGIS. From there, kernel
density estimation produced a hotspot surface showing where extremely poor
households concentrate most heavily across the city, rather than just a
count per administrative unit — a hotspot map distinguishes a few dense
pockets of deep poverty from the same total number of households spread
thinly across a wide area, and those two situations call for different
interventions.

## Profiling each sub-district

A hotspot alone doesn't say why an area is poor or what it needs. The second
half of the work profiled every sub-district against five characteristics
drawn from the P3KE dataset and supporting local statistics: employment
status, education level, housing condition, access to sanitation, and
stunting risk among children. Layering these against the hotspot surface let
the analysis distinguish, for example, a coastal sub-district where poverty
tracks closely with poor sanitation access from an inland one where the
driver looks more like informal or absent employment — distinctions that
matter for deciding whether a program should lead with infrastructure,
livelihood support, or health intervention.

## Output

The deliverable was a set of thematic maps and a sub-district profile table
handed to the city government as an input to targeting decisions —
identifying which sub-districts should be prioritized first and on what
basis, grounded in the same P3KE data the national program already collects
rather than a new survey. The value of the work was less in new data
collection and more in making data that already existed, but sat locked in
spreadsheets, visible and actionable at the sub-district level where poverty
programs actually get delivered.
