Cikarang, in Bekasi Regency, is one of the densest industrial corridors in
Southeast Asia — and also, increasingly, a place people live. Factories and
housing have grown up next to each other with very little formal spatial
separation, and the usual way of studying that (walking the area, digitizing
land parcels by hand) doesn't scale to a district this size. This thesis
asked a narrower, answerable question: where exactly do industrial and
residential building footprints concentrate, and where do the two land uses
sit close enough to each other that friction — noise, traffic, pollution
exposure — becomes likely.

## Detecting buildings from open satellite data

Rather than digitizing footprints manually, the study pulled building
polygons for the study area from Google's Open Building dataset inside
Google Earth Engine, then classified each footprint as industrial or
residential using a combination of footprint size, shape regularity, and
cross-referencing against existing land-use layers — industrial buildings in
Cikarang tend to be large, rectangular, and clustered in gated estates, which
gives a genuine visual and geometric signal to separate them from the
smaller, denser, more irregular footprints of surrounding kampung housing. A
Python pipeline handled the cleanup: removing sliver polygons, resolving
buildings split across tile boundaries, and exporting the final classified
set as vector data for analysis.

## Kernel density and nearest-neighbour distance

With both building sets extracted, the analysis had two parts. Kernel
density estimation was run separately for industrial and residential
footprints to produce continuous surfaces of where each land use
concentrates most heavily — turning a scatter of individual buildings into a
readable heat map of industrial cores and residential cores. Nearest-neighbour
distance analysis then measured, building by building, how close each
residential footprint sits to the nearest industrial one, which is where
IBM SPSS came in for the statistical side: testing whether the observed
clustering and proximity patterns were significant rather than what you'd
expect from a random distribution of buildings across the same area.

## What the pattern shows

The combined output is a spatial association map: zones where industrial and
residential density peaks overlap or sit within a short buffer of each
other, versus zones where the two land uses stay genuinely separated by
distance or by intervening land. Those overlap zones are the ones a spatial
plan would need to address first — through buffer requirements, zoning
revisions, or infrastructure that mitigates the exposure — rather than
treating industrial-residential proximity across Cikarang as a uniform
problem. As a thesis, the deliverable was the analysis and its statistical
validation rather than a shipped tool, but the same footprint-detection and
kernel-density workflow is directly reusable for any other Indonesian
industrial corridor with Open Building coverage.
